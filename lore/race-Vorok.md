# The Vorok

The Vorok are the miners of Hedoria — a wartide-blooded people organized into trading consortiums that operate ore-works in every kingdom that will sign their contracts. They are the continent's iron, copper, silver, and stone. The kingdoms dislike them and renew the contracts.

## **Origin and Form**

The Vorok are a wartide race. Their precursors were bred by the Wartide as labor — heavy bodies built to dig, haul, and break stone in the great fortifications of the dark powers. Avenoria's working caught them mid-labor and left them as people, with no orders and the pit-works collapsing around them. They organized.

  

A Vorok stands taller than a human, and the silhouette varies by trade. The body adapts to the work across a lifetime. A Vorok who spends decades at the seam-face grows broad and slab-shouldered, with thick chest and arms and a heavy jaw. A Vorok who works as foreman, quartermaster, or principal stays lean and finishes their life narrow-built. The same lineage produces both. Voroks regard this as ordinary.

  

The face is heavy-jawed, with prominent lower tusks, a broad brow, deep-set eyes, and large pointed ears that fold flat in concentration or anger. Their hair is coarse and grows in a stiff crest from crown to neck, shaved short for work or kept long for standing. Their skin runs in dull tones — grey, slate, smoky blue, dusty orange, ochre — with no consistent pattern. Two siblings can come out very different colors.

  

They live roughly as long as humans. Their voices are deep and slow, and they speak common-tongue with a grinding accent.

## **Reproduction**

There are no Vorok women. Reproduction is contractual.

  

The houses maintain breeding compounds adjoining their major operations — not pits despite the laborer slang, but decent housing with kitchens, gardens, and medical staff. The compounds are staffed by women under fixed-term contracts of bearing. The standard contract specifies term length, frequency of coupling, number of bearings expected, accommodation, medical provision, payment schedule, and release terms. Voroks are not very fertile, and frequent coupling is expected — the contracts account for it explicitly, and the rates reflect it. Most contracted bearers are Quelled, sold to Vorok houses through the southern markets where the trade is legal. A smaller number are women of other races — humans down on their luck, the occasional Threshi dissident, Hofnar in catastrophic debt — who sign on for the high rates the work commands. A few seek the work directly. A successful contract is paid out accordingly.

  

The houses do not advertise the arrangement and do not discuss it with foreign clients. It is not secret in any meaningful sense — anyone who asks is told the basic structure — but it is not a feature of the brand. There are persistent rumors of less voluntary contracts, of bearers held past their term, of compounds in the deeper galleries where the formal terms are not honored. The houses deny these rumors. Some are denied more convincingly than others.

  

Laborers take their turns in rotation. A turn is paid leave from the seam — decent food, decent rest, and the work the body is otherwise denied. The houses regard the rotation as operationally essential. Voroks deprived of their turns get rowdy fast. Foremen who run their crews short on rotation lose them to the next house that doesn't.

  

A Vorok child is raised in the compound until adolescence and is then placed with a house operation as an apprentice. He has no parents in the human sense. He has the house.

## **The Homeland**

The Vorok homeland is a stretch of broken mountain country in the central interior — the territory where they emerged from the working. It was the richest ore country on the continent for two centuries, and it is largely mined out. The consortiums extracted what was easy, then what was hard, then moved their active operations to foreign ground.

  

The communities left behind are poor and lawless. Consortium attention has withdrawn, and no central authority has replaced it. Disputes are settled by the local strong, by old custom, or by violence. Free crews and bandit bands pass through and sometimes settle. The houses keep their old mountain capitals in repair as ceremonial seats and visit rarely.

## **The Houses**

Vorok civilization is the consortiums. There is no nation, no king, no council. There are seven great houses and dozens of lesser ones, and a Vorok belongs to a house the way a citizen of another race belongs to a kingdom. The house owns his contract, houses him, feeds him, pays him hourly, and represents him in any dealing with another house or with foreign powers. A Vorok without a house affiliation is no one.

  

Houses are run by principals — senior Voroks who have accumulated meaningful ownership in the consortium. Principals are not elected, inherited, or appointed. They buy in. A junior Vorok who saves carefully, invests shrewdly, and acquires a sufficient share-block becomes a voice in the house's direction. Enough share and he is a principal. Enough principals agreeing and the house moves. Incompetent principals lose their share-blocks within a few years and cease to be principals.

  

Houses compete ferociously and cooperate where the work requires it. There are inter-house compacts on contract pricing, on dispute resolution, and on the management of the homeland communities they have collectively neglected. Open warfare between houses would damage the brand. Maneuvering, undercutting, and quiet sabotage are continuous.

  

Within a house, men know each other across decades. Foremen, crew-bosses, and the laborers under them work together for years and develop the loyalties that long association produces. Senior Voroks have proteges they have brought up through the operations, and principals have rivals on the share-block they have spent careers maneuvering against. These are the relationships of Vorok life. They run through the house, not around it.

## **The Work**

Voroks are the best miners and prospecting geologists on the continent.

  

A Vorok pit-foreman knows ground-water, gas pockets, fault-lines, ore-body structure, ventilation, and tunnel-shoring at a level no foreign engineer matches. The houses keep this knowledge proprietary. They do not publish, do not teach foreign apprentices, and do not let senior foremen take work outside the house.

  

A trained Vorok prospector can also feel ore beneath the ground — depth, direction, approximate richness, the structure it sits in. The sense is not flashy and has no combat application. It is just useful for the one thing it does. A Vorok prospector surveys in a week what a human team surveys in a year, and his estimate is more accurate. The houses charge accordingly.

## **The Contract Is the World**

Voroks are transactional. A Vorok pit-laborer is paid by the hour, audited weekly, and presented with a statement of account. He understands the statement. He has signed similar statements his entire working life. Vorok labor contracts run to many pages and account for hours, output, injury rates, equipment provision, room and board, advances, penalties, and termination. Foreign clients accustomed to looser arrangements find them tedious. Voroks find this objection incomprehensible.

  

A Vorok who likes you quotes you a fair price. A Vorok who does not like you quotes a high one. There is no other register.

## **Manner**

Voroks are crude. Their humor is rough and physical, their language plainly obscene by the standards of any other race, and their idea of pleasant company involves volume, drink, and the sort of jokes that would empty a Cephalen reception in under a minute. A pit-crew at the end of a shift is loud. A pit-crew off-shift in a house tavern is louder. Foreign clients who visit a Vorok operation are kept on the principal's side of the compound, where the manner is adjusted for the room.

  

Independent Voroks — free crews and exiles — carry the same register without the house polish, and a free Vorok in a foreign town is recognizable by the swagger as much as the silhouette. A woman who comes within easy speaking distance of a free Vorok will, more often than not, find herself offered a short-term contract on the spot. The terms are spelled out plainly and the rates are competitive.

## **The Free Crews**

Not every Vorok is in a house. A laborer between contracts, a foreman who quit, an apprentice who refused his first indenture — these exist, and they do not always find their way into another house's books. They band together as free crews, working independent claims in marginal country, hiring out for short jobs the houses won't touch, occasionally prospecting for veins they hope to sell to a house at premium.

  

Many free crews drift into banditry. The skills are transferable — a crew that can blast a tunnel can blast a road. Vorok bandits are a recognized hazard along the old homeland frontiers and in the back country between kingdoms. The houses do not pursue them and do not consider unenrolled Voroks their problem. Foreign authorities that complain are quoted on the cost of contracted security.

  

A small number of free crews stay independent and honest, operating at small scale on work the houses scorn. They are tolerated. The houses regard them as a species of failure.

## **Exiles**

Vorok exile is rare. A house that wants a Vorok genuinely expunged from the system can revoke his enrollment and share his name across the inter-house compacts. The blacklisted Vorok cannot work in any consortium operation anywhere on the continent. Free crews will sometimes take such a one. Most exiles leave Vorok society entirely and take work in the wider world — caravan guards, mining-camp consultants for foreign operations the houses have not reached, occasional bodyguards for foreign principals who want a Vorok's particular register without the complication of contracting with a house.
