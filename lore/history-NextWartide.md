The next Wartide

This is where the world becomes genuinely tense.

The Wartide was broken, not destroyed. The Hollows where its fragments concentrated are not stable — they are, slowly, growing. The unredeemed creatures of the wild places are more numerous than they were a generation ago. The frontier raids are more frequent and better organized than they used to be. Hunters and rangers along the frontier all see it. Border villages all feel it. Reports come in to capitals and are filed.

Most people are not paying attention. Most people are paying attention to the soul-war that everyone can see coming. Tensions between the southern human kingdoms and the Raknid holds have been escalating for a decade. The wizard organizations are openly war-talking against Avenor. Hofnar-human relations are at their worst in a century. Molvar defensive postures along the great forest borders are visible to anyone who looks. The third soul-war is coming, and every diplomatic energy on the continent is bent toward stopping it or winning it.

While this is happening, the second Wartide is gathering. The Hollows are expanding. The corruption is reaching out. The unredeemed are moving in numbers no one has seen since the original war. Frontier scouts who report the scale of what they're seeing are dismissed — bureaucratic skepticism, political inconvenience, the simple problem that no one in power wants to deal with two crises at once and the soul-war is the visible one. The reports pile up. They are filed. They are not acted on.

This is the Scourge / White Walker move, and it works as well in Hedoria as it works anywhere. The audience-level dramatic irony is gigantic: the players know, the world doesn't, and the gap between those two is most of what makes the setting feel urgent.

A few features that make this work specifically:

The frontier is where the truth lives. Frontier rangers, hunters, the rougher Hofnar families, certain Fernwarg clans, the Raknid holds (especially the ones nearest the deepest Hollows), backwater Molvar groves, low-caste Metalite mining settlements — these are the people who are seeing it firsthand. Their reports are dismissed by every metropolitan power. A PC who spends time on the frontier cannot fail to see the evidence. A PC who only operates in capitals can go a long time without taking it seriously.

Some powers know and don't say. The Primes, of course, know — they are watching it more carefully than anyone, in their patient way, and they are not telling. (Their reasoning: it is not their war to fight. Same as last time. The shame compounds.) Avenor knows in fragments — the witch queen's intelligence service is the best on the continent, and Avenor's frontier monitoring is genuinely competitive — but Avenor is exhausted by the soul-war prevention work and cannot turn its attention to a second crisis without abandoning the first. Avenor's quietest fear is that they will choose wrong: focus on the soul-war and miss the Wartide, or focus on the Wartide and lose the soul-war. The witch queen's court is having this argument right now and not winning it.

Some powers know and are doing the wrong thing. The wizard organizations have noticed Wartide activity but are interpreting it wrong — they read it as evidence that the original ensoulment was incomplete and that the proper response is more aggressive control of magic, more witch-killing, more enforcement. This is not just stupid; it's a doctrine that makes the problem worse because it weakens the only forces (Avenor, the witches, the cooperative non-human magical traditions) that might actually help against a Wartide. Some of the wizard organizations' senior figures know this and don't care, because their priority is institutional dominance, not the world.

The southern human kingdoms know and don't believe. Reports of frontier creatures are dismissed in the southern courts as exaggerations or as enemy propaganda from Avenor. The southern kingdoms are also preparing for the soul-war and treat any non-soul-war intelligence as distraction.

The Halfshorn lineages, when we name them, mostly know. They are descended from the Wartide. They feel its return in their blood. Some are terrified — the Raknid especially, who fought it once and who would lose everything if it returned and could not be turned back. Some are hopeful in the awful sense — the most degraded Halfshorn lineages may welcome the second Wartide as a chance for reunion with what they were. This is one of the great moral tests for the Halfshorn: when the Wartide rises again, what do you do?

The unredeemed precursors are moving in the same direction. The Old Green who hate Molvar, the Deep Kin who hate high-caste Metalites, certain wolf-packs that have been seen acting organized in ways wolves should not act — these are leading edges of the same phenomenon. The Wartide doesn't move alone. It pulls on what was always there beneath civilization, and the wild things stir.

The signs are subtle until they aren't. Animals behaving strangely. A river running the wrong color for a week. A village on the frontier that goes silent and is not heard from. A Hollow that was the size of a county a decade ago is now the size of three. A specific kind of dream that more and more people are having. The corruption builds slowly until it doesn't.

What this gives the setting

A two-layered crisis structure. The visible crisis (the soul-war) is what everyone is fighting about. The actual crisis (the Wartide) is what will end the world if it isn't stopped. PCs operating at any level get to engage with both — soul-war politics if they care about that, frontier work if they want to confront the real thing. Most campaigns will weave between them. The soul-war provides the political texture and the named villains; the Wartide provides the cosmic stakes and the slow-building dread.

A reason for adventure parties to exist. Mixed-race PC groups make sense in a Wartide context the way they don't in pure soul-war politics. Soul-war politics push races apart; Wartide threats push races together. A frontier party that has actually seen Wartide creatures has more in common with each other than with their respective home kingdoms. This is a great structural reason for the kind of adventuring company Voyage's RPG mode wants to support.

A narrative arc for the world. Voyage campaigns can play in any era, but the current moment of the setting is one of mounting tension on two axes. Players can be involved in soul-war prevention, soul-war fighting, frontier defense, Wartide investigation, or the slow political work of getting the metropolitan powers to take it seriously. Different campaigns at the same table can be addressing different facets of the same world-historical crisis.

A way for player choices to matter at scale. A campaign that succeeds in averting the third soul-war, a campaign that destabilizes a Hollow, a campaign that exposes the Prime archives, a campaign that brings a Halfshorn lineage to the right side — any of these can shift the world's trajectory. The world is on a knife's edge and individual actions can change the outcome. This is excellent design for an RPG.

A specific tone of dread. The setting is not "dark and grimy fantasy where everyone is awful." It is "lively and lived-in fantasy where most life is good but the second Wartide is coming and most of the people in power are not paying attention." That's a much more interesting tone — it preserves the warmth and camaraderie and ordinary good life that you want at the table-level, while keeping the cosmic stakes immense. The original Larion worldBackground emphasizes that most of life is good and that camaraderie matters; this setup preserves that exactly while adding the dread underneath.

Updated picture

The world's central facts are now:

  

The Wartide came centuries ago. Avenoria broke it through the working (the ensoulment), which created the new races as wartime allies. Humanity survived but lost most of its world. The new races took the lands they had defended.

Two soul-wars have been fought since over the post-war settlement. The third is brewing.

The Primes withdrew during the Wartide and did not help. They preserve their knowledge and their shame in their mountain monasteries. Avenor knows and does not say.

The second Wartide is coming. Frontier evidence is mounting. Most powers are not taking it seriously, distracted by the soul-war, dismissing the reports, or actively making the situation worse.

Avenor knows in fragments and is overstretched. The Primes know fully and are not saying. The wizard organizations are misreading the signs and worsening the response. The Halfshorn lineages feel it coming and are divided about what to do.
