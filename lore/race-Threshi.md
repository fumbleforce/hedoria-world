# The Threshi

The Threshi are the locust-people of central Hedoria — a wartide-race that holds the fertile lowlands and river-country at the heart of the continent. They are the most numerous people in Hedoria, breed rapidly, and have more mouths than their farmland can feed in any but the best years. Their unattached males ride out at every season's end to take more land and meat from the human kingdoms at their borders, and have done so for three centuries.

## **Origin and Form**

The Threshi were lifted in the Ensoulment from the bulk hosts the Wartide had bred for its campaigns. When the working caught them, they were holding the lowland river-country the Wartide had taken from the human kingdoms. Some of the Wartide hosts the working touched broke under it and turned against their own — those are the Raknid. The Threshi did neither. The Wartide's command no longer reached them, but its appetites did. They kept the land they were standing on and began to farm it.

  

The form is locust and mantis both, leaning locust. An adult Threshi stands taller than a human, narrow-bodied, chitin-plated in dust-browns, tans, pale ochres, and sand-yellows. The head is triangular, with large compound eyes, mandibles that fold inward when they speak and outward when they eat, and antennae trailing back along the skull. The wings are segmented panels folded along the back at rest, capable of short flights and long glides rather than sustained flight. War-bands cover ground partly on foot and partly in the air.

## **Male and Female**

The Threshi are sexually dimorphic, and their work is divided by biology.

  

Females are larger and heavier, broad through the shoulders, with powerful forelimbs ending in chitin shovels that break ground no human plow could turn. They plow, sow, weed, run irrigation, tend the nursery-mounds, and lay the eggs.

  

Males are leaner and faster, narrower-shouldered, with forelimbs folded into curved sickle-blades that unfold in motion. They harvest. The harvest-scythe and the war-scythe are the same instrument, but a Threshi male is a farmer with sharp arms, not a trained soldier.

  

Females lay clutches of twenty to forty eggs several times a year, in nursery-mounds tended by the older females of the household. The young hatch fast, grow fast, and are working in the fields within their first year. Mortality is high; replacement is faster. Considerably more males hatch than females.

  

A female with plowed land can build a household if she secures a male's labor at harvest. A male without a female and her land has nothing of his own. The kingdom is therefore always carrying a surplus of unattached males. Some take work in the cities or at the King's court — scribes, builders, smiths, artisans. Some attach to a noble's retinue. Most ride out at season's end to take what land they can.

  

Most of the landless die in a campaign or two. The few who survive several seasons become formidable, having learned what kills a young Threshi in human country and how to use the scythe-arms against an enemy that fights back. They are kept on by warlords as veteran retainers or ride independently as professional raiders. The kingdom has no standing army.

## **The Cycle**

While the fields are growing, the females work them. While the harvest is being cut, the males are at home. Once the harvest is in, the unattached males have nothing to do until next year's grain ripens, and they turn outward.

  

Some go in organized bands under a warlord, on whatever business the kingdom directs — settling disputes between noble houses, pressing on a contested border, taking the strips of land next year's plowing will incorporate. Many more go in small groups or alone, seeking land they can hold and a female who will join them on it.

  

Some go for Quelled. The Threshi need meat and have never managed to keep livestock — any household that tries to raise Quelled has its herd stolen and eaten by other Threshi within a season. Raids for Quelled are about as common as raids for land. Human kingdoms at the Threshi border lose pens and breeding stock as routinely as they lose villages.

  

Spring and summer are quiet. Autumn tenses with the harvest. Winter brings the bands. By spring they have fallen back to plow.

  

Any single band can be broken by a competent human force; organized resistance scatters them. But the bands are continuous, and the season does not end. Three centuries of inches has put a great quantity of formerly human land under Threshi grain. Significant territory is still in active dispute.

## **Hierarchy**

The Threshi are aristocratic and hierarchical, organized around warlord-houses. The King is always male, always a great warrior, ruling from the central capital. Succession passes by formal challenge or dynastic claim, and no Threshi king has died peacefully.

  

Each great noble-house is led by a male warlord and his consort-females, who manage the agricultural side of the holding. The warlord cannot field his war-band without the harvest, and the consort-females cannot bring the harvest in without male labor at cutting time. Both sides hold real power within the same house.

  

Below the nobility are the lesser families and the field-workers. Mobility is limited. A male who distinguishes himself in war can be raised into a retinue. A female who runs an extraordinary harvest can be granted larger land.

  

The court keeps specialists for bureaucracy, scribing, and foreign relations. Threshi envoys at human courts are usually older males whose campaigns are behind them.

## **The Closed Doors**

The Threshi do not trade meaningfully with the other races. They produce what they consume, take by raid what they cannot grow, and store what is left. Foreign visitors are not permitted past the border-zones. There are no Hofnar trading posts inside Threshi territory, no Cephalen embassies, no human merchant houses. The few diplomatic contacts happen at border crossings — prisoner exchanges, formal protests, occasional ransoms.

## **Dissent and the Risings**

The field-workers rise periodically against their lords. Risings start local — a hold-master killed, a granary opened, a noble's retinue cut down — and spread. At their largest they have engulfed whole regions before being put down. Loyalist war-bands from other regions are mobilized against rebel ones. Leaders are executed publicly. The fields go back to work. The next rising comes a generation later.

## **The Threshi Abroad**

A Threshi outside the kingdom is almost always an envoy on formal business, escorted and returning soon.

  

A smaller population exists outside that frame: dissidents who fled a warlord, escaped a failed rising, or could not bear the life they were hatched into. Most reach Avenor under the queen's writ. They are recognizable on sight — chitin-bodied, work-arms carried awkwardly, in human clothes they have not learned to wear well — and they take what labor their forelimbs can manage.
