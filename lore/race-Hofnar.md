## **The Hofnar**

The Hofnar are the great traders of Hedoria — the equine-humanoid people of the open steppes, whose painted wagon-cities cross the continent in long migratory routes and whose merchants pass through every port, market, and frontier town that other races have built. They are the connective tissue of Hedoria's economy, the people whose presence makes overland trade possible across kingdoms that would otherwise refuse to deal with one another. Where Hofnar caravans go, goods follow. Where they refuse to go, the world stops.

### **Origin and Form**

The Hofnar were lifted in the Ensoulment from the great wild herds of the steppes — aurochs, wild horses, and the larger grazing beasts that ran the open lands before Avenoria's working. They emerged from the Ensoulment in humanoid form: tall, broad-shouldered, mane-haired, and hoof-toed, with the build of a people made for distance and weather. An adult Hofnar stands a head taller than a human on average, with longer limbs, thicker bones, and the kind of casual physical durability that comes from a lineage built for running.

Their faces are recognizably their own — broader than human, with prominent cheekbones, wide-set dark eyes, and a slight muzzle that is not quite a snout but is unmistakably non-human. Some lineages bear small horns at the temples; others have none. Their hair grows as a true mane, falling from the top of the head down the back of the neck, and is often elaborately braided, beaded, or bound according to clan tradition. Their feet are hoofed — split-hooved in some lineages, single-hooved in others — and they walk with a slightly different gait than races built for flat-soled feet, faster across open ground and more careful on stairs.

Their unensouled cousins, the wild Far-Herds, still range the deepest interior steppes — vast wild aurochs and proto-horse-folk that the Hofnar regard with religious reverence as the Grandfathers and never hunt or harm.

### **The Wagon-Cities**

The Hofnar do not build fixed cities. Their civilization moves. The capital of any Hofnar confederation is a great wagon-city — hundreds of vast wagons drawn by oxen-equivalents and arranged in formation when the city is encamped, with central avenues of beaten earth, market squares, ceremonial fires, and the embroidered tents of clan dignitaries. A Hofnar capital at rest is unmistakable on the horizon: bright with banners, painted in clan colors, ordered with a precision that surprises foreign visitors who expect nomad camps to be chaotic.

The wagons themselves are works of generations. A great clan-wagon may be the size of a small castle, its sides carved and painted with the deeds of the clan's ancestors, its interior lined with hide, felt, and embroidered hangings, its furniture built to fold and travel. The wealthiest wagons have multiple levels, carved verandas, and small gilt fittings on their yokes. A clan's wagon is its hall, its temple, its archive, and its history; to lose a clan-wagon is the most catastrophic possible blow to a Hofnar lineage, and clans that have lost their wagons spend generations rebuilding standing.

The wagon-cities follow the grass. Hofnar capitals migrate on long seasonal cycles — summering in the high northern steppes, wintering in the southern grasslands, with predictable encampments at established sites along the route. Foreign envoys and trading partners know where the great Hofnar capitals will be in any given season and travel to meet them there. Smaller Hofnar communities — trading posts, way-stations, individual family caravans — operate more flexibly, but the great clan-wagons keep to their cycles.

### **Society**

Hofnar society is organized by clan, with each clan headed by an elder council of senior matriarchs and patriarchs. The clans are confederated into larger groupings called Riderings — the historical alliances that share migration routes, intermarry, and conduct common business. There are seven major Riderings recognized across the steppes, each with its own traditions, dialect, and customary territories, and dozens of smaller bands that operate within or alongside them.

Within the clan, status is built on three things: lineage, deeds, and *standing in trade*. A young Hofnar of common birth who proves themselves a brilliant trader can rise to clan leadership in a generation; a high-born Hofnar who fails their commercial obligations falls accordingly. The Hofnar measure of a person is what they have built and what they have honored — old promises kept, debts repaid, contracts honored across decades. A Hofnar whose word is reliable is a Hofnar in good standing. A Hofnar who has broken a contract is something less, and the news travels.

Family life is communal and public. Hofnar children are raised within the clan as much as within the immediate family, and the elders of the clan play a major role in upbringing. Children learn to ride and to drive wagons before they learn to read; they learn the trade-routes and the contracts before they learn formal letters. By adolescence a Hofnar is expected to know the major roads of the continent, the customs of the major foreign powers, and the basic reckoning of currencies and goods. Hofnar education is thoroughly *practical* — abstract scholarship is admired but not pursued, and the great philosophical schools of Hedoria have few Hofnar students.

### **The Trade Network**

The Hofnar control overland trade across Hedoria the way the Cephalen control the seas, but their position is different in character. Where the Cephalen took their monopoly through blockade and force, the Hofnar earned theirs through reliability and reach. Hofnar caravans go where no one else's will — through the dangerous frontier regions, across hostile borders, into kingdoms that refuse to deal with each other directly. They are *neutral by reputation*, paying tribute where required, observing local customs scrupulously, taking no sides in the wars and feuds of the lands they cross. They survive by being indispensable to everyone and aligned with no one.

Hofnar trading houses extend across the continent. The great clans maintain trading posts at every major junction, employ agents in every important city, and run regular caravans on schedules that have been kept for generations. Hofnar contracts are famously detailed — the standard Hofnar contract of carriage runs to many pages, accounting for weather, banditry, war, taxation, spoilage, and the dozen other contingencies of long-distance trade. A contract well-drafted by a Hofnar agent can be enforced in any Hedorian court that recognizes contract law, and most do.

This neutrality is not without cost. The Hofnar deal with the chattel kingdoms — buying their grain and metal, selling them goods from other regions — and they participate in the chattel trade itself, transporting Quelled to and from the southern markets. This is one of the moral compromises of the Hofnar position, and it is increasingly contested within the Riderings. Progressive Hofnar families refuse the chattel contracts and have done so for a generation; traditional families take the work as ordinary commerce. The split runs through clans, through marriages, sometimes through individual conscience. A formal split between progressive and traditional Riderings has been predicted for years and has not yet come.
