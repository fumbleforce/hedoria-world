# The Fernwarg

The Fernwarg are the wolf-and-fern people of the central forests of Hedoria — the pack-social, matriarchal predators of the open woodland and the forest-edge country, whose territories run alongside and partly within the deeper woods held by the Raknid, and whose long political dispute with the human kingdoms over the forests they share has not yet broken into a third soul-war but is among the pressures pushing toward one. They are forest-floor hunters, mistaken at distance for green-furred wolves on their hindlegs, and not quite that on closer inspection.

## **Origin and Form**

The Fernwarg were lifted in the Ensoulment from the forest wolves of the central woodlands — the great pack-predators of pre-Wartide Hedoria, hunters of deer and boar through the deep woods, animals already half-mythical in the human stories of that era. Avenoria's working caught them along with everything else that ran the forests in those final terrible years, and what emerged was not quite wolves and not quite humans but something the woods themselves seemed to have grown.

  

A Fernwarg stands upright, two-legged and tall — taller than a human but not as broad — with the long-limbed proportions of a runner rather than a wrestler. Their faces are unmistakably wolf-derived: long muzzle, forward-set amber or yellow eyes, ears erect and mobile, jaws still built for the bite. The expression-register is canid, and a human can read a Fernwarg's mood at a glance — the lifted lip, the pinned ears, the bared teeth — in a way the more alien races of Hedoria don't permit. This makes the Fernwarg seem more knowable than they are. It is one of the regular sources of human miscalculation in the forests.

  

Their hands are clawed and long-fingered, capable of fine work but ending in genuine hooked claws they do not bother to hide. Their feet are digitigrade, padded, with shorter claws built for traction on forest floor.

  

The coat is the thing that names them. Fernwarg fur grows in fronded layers along the back, shoulders, arms, and tail — not actual fern leaves but a fur that has taken on the structure and color of fronds, fine-divided and overlapping, in shades of deep green, moss, and forest-dark. The undercoat is denser fur of more conventional kind. The fern-coat shifts with the seasons: lush green in spring and summer, browning and russet in autumn, sparser and darker in winter when the undergrowth does the same. A Fernwarg standing still in summer bracken is invisible to anything that isn't another Fernwarg. A human scout learns this once, generally fatally.

  

The mane runs from the high shoulders down the spine and is the most elaborate part of the coat — a thick fern-growth that lifts in threat and display, woven by its bearer with bones, claws, beads, and the marks of significant hunts. The mane's size and elaboration are the immediate visual reading of a Fernwarg's standing. A young female has a thin mane; an established hunter has a fuller one; a pack-mother carries something approaching a headdress of layered fronds, dark and dense and unmistakable from across a clearing.

## **Female and Male**

The Fernwarg are matriarchal in the structural sense — not by custom but by anatomy. Females are larger than males, heavier-built, more muscular, with dense manes and the physical authority that follows from those facts. A male Fernwarg is leaner, lighter, faster on the chase and quicker through brush, but smaller. A pack-mother in her prime stands a head taller than the males around her and weighs half again as much. The arrangement is not contested because it cannot be.

  

Female anatomy carries further consequences. Fernwarg females mate and give birth through the same channel — a difficult, dangerous biology that makes a young female's first birth the central passage of her life. First births are attended by the pack-mother and the elder females, take long hours, and are not always survived. A Fernwarg woman who has borne and lived is a Fernwarg woman who has earned standing. Pack-mothers are women who have borne and lived more than once, and the dense manes they wear are partly the literal record of those passages — bones and beads woven into the fronds in numbers any other Fernwarg can read at a glance.

  

Mating is the female's choice. Males display, court, present themselves, and are accepted or rejected on the female's judgment. The courtship customs of the wider continent — where men pursue and women are pursued — strike the Fernwarg as backwards when they bother to think about it. A male Fernwarg dealing with a human woman is sometimes deferential in ways she does not understand. A female Fernwarg dealing with a human man is sometimes amused at his approach in ways that read, to him, as rudeness.

## **The Pack**

A Fernwarg pack is an extended matriline — a pack-mother, her daughters and granddaughters with their offspring, the males who have joined the pack through the females' choosing, and the children of the line. Packs run from a dozen to fifty, depending on territory and season. Larger groupings — the great forest-confederations — are alliances of pack-mothers, formed through long custom and through the exchange of male sons sent to other packs.

  

Succession passes through the female line. The pack-mother's eldest daughter is the presumptive heir, but daughters of other lineages may challenge, and the actual transfer of authority happens through some combination of physical contest, demonstrated wisdom, and the support of the elder females. The contest is rarely fatal — the Fernwarg do not waste pack-mother stock — but it is decisive, and the loser accepts the outcome.

  

Males within the pack are hunters, fathers, and sometimes the pack-mother's chosen consorts, and they are honored for their work. They are not contenders for leadership. The path of advancement that exists for a Hofnar or a Draklid male — winning standing through deeds, building it across a lifetime, eventually leading — does not exist for a male Fernwarg in his birth-pack. The most ambitious male in the world will never lead the pack he was born to. The matriarchy is not symbolic.

  

This produces the Fernwarg males who travel. A younger son with no path to standing at home, a brother who lost his mother's favor, a male whose female contemporaries chose other consorts — these leave, and they leave often. Fernwarg males appear across the continent as scouts, mercenaries, oath-bound retainers, occasional gamekeepers in human estates that have made their peace with the local pack. They are recognizable: tall lean canids with green-fern coats and clawed hands, polite in the formal manner of someone who has been raised not to assume his standing in a room. They send a portion of their earnings home. They visit when they can. Some return with means and find themselves welcomed back as established consorts to pack-mothers who remember them. Others do not return.

## **Voice**

Fernwarg vocalize in two registers, and visitors to their territory remember both.

  

The howls are the public voice — long-carrying calls that pass between packs across miles of forest, pack-mothers calling to pack-mothers, hunters announcing their position to the line. The howl is wolf-derived but more articulated than any wolf's, and a fluent Fernwarg can carry meaning across distance in ways that ordinary listeners hear as merely song.

  

The other voice is for the pack at home. It is closer to a hyena's whoop than a wolf's call — a chorus of rising laughs, whoops, and chittering that erupts from a Fernwarg camp at evening when the pack is together and at ease. Human travelers who have heard it from outside the firelight describe it the same way: the sound of something laughing in pieces from several places at once. It is not unfriendly. It is the sound of a pack that has eaten well and is talking among itself. The Fernwarg do not understand why outsiders find it disturbing, but they notice that they do, and they are not always sorry.

## **The Forest Compact**

The Fernwarg share their forests with the Raknid, and have done so since both peoples emerged from the Ensoulment. The arrangement is older than any treaty either could write. The Fernwarg hold the open woodland, the forest-edge, the brush-country and the river-valleys where the canopy thins. The Raknid hold the deep old-growth, the shadowed valleys where the canopy closes overhead and the ground grows damp and dim. The two ranges meet in long borderlands of mixed wood that neither claims and both pass through.

  

The compact is unwritten and observed. Fernwarg packs do not run prey into deep Raknid country. Raknid hunters do not descend into open woodland for game. At the borderlands, agreed paths and customary signs mark the courtesies — Fernwarg territory-markers the Raknid recognize, Raknid web-marks the Fernwarg respect. A Fernwarg who finds a Raknid sign on a tree turns aside without comment. A Raknid who finds a Fernwarg pack-mark on a stream takes the long way around. There has been no war between them in living memory.

  

This does not mean alliance. The two peoples are not friends. They tolerate each other because their niches do not seriously overlap and because both remember what happened to the human armies that tried to take their forests in the First Soul-War. They reinforce one another by existing — a force that pushes through Fernwarg country finds itself in Raknid country if it presses too far, and a force that descends on Raknid country finds itself in Fernwarg country on the way out. Outsiders who have learned this from experience speak of the forests as if they were a single power. They are not. They are two powers who have agreed that neither of them is the human kingdoms' problem to solve.

  

The overlap is in prey. Both races hunt deer, boar, and the larger forest game, and both have the run of borderlands where these animals move freely. Tense moments come when a Fernwarg pack and a Raknid hunting party converge on the same herd. The protocol holds: whichever party reached the prey first has the claim, and the other turns aside. It is not always clean. There are bad seasons in lean years. The pack-mothers and the Raknid elders meet at the border-stones once a year to walk the routes and confirm the arrangement, and most of what goes wrong gets corrected at those meetings rather than between them.

## **The Human Question**

The Fernwarg hold the great central forests, and the human kingdoms at their borders want what those forests contain — timber, game, charcoal, river access, the lower meadows for grazing — and want it on their own terms. The dispute has been ongoing since the First Soul-War, when human armies attempted to push into the forests and were broken in territory they did not understand against an enemy they could not see. The forests have been Fernwarg ever since.

  

Three centuries on, the pressure has not gone away. Border lords push their woodcutters past the agreed lines. Hunting parties cross into pack territory and are sometimes never seen again. Logging companies bribe local officials for permits the Fernwarg have not granted. Each generation tests the borders, and each generation is reminded what the forests are.

  

The Fernwarg respond politically. They send delegations to human courts — pack-mothers in their formal dress, accompanied by chosen consorts and sometimes by their daughters being introduced to politics — and they negotiate, hard, on the questions that matter to them. Border treaties. Logging rights. Pursuit of Quelled escapees who have crossed into the forests, which the Fernwarg refuse to return. Hunting rights along contested rivers. They are difficult interlocutors. They do not soften their positions to make humans comfortable. They are not in a hurry. They have the territory and the time, and they speak from both.

  

The pressure for a third soul-war comes partly from this front. Human nationalist movements push hard for renewed expansion into the forests. The Fernwarg pack-mothers, in turn, have been quietly coordinating with the Raknid elders for the first time in a century. Neither forest people wants the war. Both are preparing for it. The pack-mothers' delegations to Avenor have grown longer and more frequent in the last decade, and the witch queen's diplomatic corps treats them with the seriousness they deserve.

## **The Fernwarg Abroad**

A Fernwarg encountered outside the forests is almost always male, almost always young, and almost always working. Mercenary companies value them — fast in pursuit, terrible in close combat, and possessed of the absolute reliability that comes from a male trained from cubhood to honor the contracts his pack makes for him. Caravan guards, scouts, bodyguards in noble households, occasional duelists. They charge fairly, work hard, and disappear when the contract ends.

  

They are recognizable on sight. Tall and lean, green-fern coats darker and shorter than they would be at home, wolfish faces with the polite reserve of someone who has learned to be careful with humans. They wear human-style clothing over the coat — tunics and breeches and traveling cloaks — and carry weapons sized for their reach. They speak common-tongue with the slight rolling cadence the Fernwarg muzzle gives every language.

  

They send money home. They speak warmly of their pack-mothers and their sisters, and rarely of the females their own age. They do not, when asked, explain the laugh.
