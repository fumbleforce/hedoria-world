# The Primes

The Primes are the elders of Hedoria — the only race on the continent that was already a people when Avenoria began her work, and the only living tradition that remembers the Ensoulment as something other than scripture or legend. They are not a great power in the manner of the Cephalen or the Hofnar. They hold no trade routes, project no fleets, and do not contend for territory beyond their mountain holdings. What they have is older. They have memory, ancestor-magic, and the bones of every Prime mage who has ever lived, kept and consulted across thousands of years. The wizard colleges are afraid of them. Avenor has historically treated them as peers. They live at the base of cold mountains in monasteries that have stood since before the modern world began, and they keep their dead.

## **Origin and Form**

The Primes pre-date the human gender-magic split. Their magic was already old when humans were still figuring theirs out, and whatever cosmic logic drove the human tradition to fracture into the wizard and witch lines does not apply to a race that was magically self-sufficient before humans had wizards. By the time of the Wartide they were ancient. The question of what they were before — what working ensouled them, in what era, by whose hand — is not one they answer to outsiders. The histories of the other races begin at the Ensoulment. The history of the Primes runs further back than that, and they are not in any hurry to share it.

  

A Prime stands taller than a human, with the heavy build of a creature evolved for cold and altitude. Their bodies are bear-like through the trunk and limbs, broad-shouldered and long-armed, covered in dense white fur that thickens further at the chest, neck, and tail. Their faces are pale and expressive, closer in proportion to a chimpanzee's than a bear's, but with a long lion-like muzzle that gives the face its distinctive forward sweep. They have long fully-furred tails, used for balance on mountain stone and for emphasis in conversation. Their hands are five-fingered and dexterous, capable of fine work; their feet are broad, padded, built for snow.

  

They robe themselves brightly — deep blue, ochre, vermilion, gold — in heavy embroidered fabrics layered against the cold. The robes are functional but also archival: clan, monastery, and lineage are written into the embroidery, and a Prime who knows what they are looking at can read another's standing from across a courtyard. Set into the robes at the throat, the chest, the wrists, and the belt are small relics — bone, tooth, fragment of jaw — bound in worked metal and placed against the body. These are the ancestors the Prime travels with.

## **The Monasteries**

The Primes do not build cities. They build monasteries.

  

The greatest of these are cut into the cliff-faces at the base of the high cold mountains, terraced upward into the rock over thousands of years of patient work, their outer walls carved with the deeds of ancestors and the names of every senior Prime whose bones rest within. A great monastery from below is a city of pillars, banners, painted facades, and skull-iconography — the bones of the honored dead displayed in the architecture itself, set into shrines and reliquaries that anchor the building to its memory. The light at altitude is sharp and the wind is constant. Banners crack in it day and night.

  

Inside, the monastery is layered. The outer halls are public — receiving rooms for envoys, libraries, study-cells, the long colonnaded galleries where junior Primes train. Deeper in are the private chambers and the working-halls where ancestor-rites are performed. Deepest of all are the bone-vaults: chambers cut directly into the mountain, climate-stable by their depth, where the ancestral remains of the monastery's lineage are kept in ordered arrangement. The truly extraordinary — the senior mages of past generations, the ancestors whose memory reaches farthest back — are interred in the great cliff-vaults, separate sanctuaries that have stood since before the Wartide and whose contents predate every other institution on the continent.

  

There are seven great monasteries currently active. There were more before the Unmade era; some were destroyed and their bones scattered, and the recovery of those lost vaults is a multi-generational project the tradition pursues with patient seriousness across centuries. Below the great monasteries are the smaller ones — family ossuaries, lineage-shrines, the working-houses of individual masters and their students. A Prime mage of any standing has access not only to their own family's bones but to the deeper ancestral lines preserved in the great vaults, if their relationship with the senior masters permits it.

## **Ancestor Magic**

Magic for a Prime is the slow conversation with the dead.

  

To work it, a mage invokes a specific ancestor — by name, with the right honorifics, the right offerings, the right preparation. The ancestor lends will and knowledge to the working. A Prime asking a long-dead astronomer for help with a calculation receives a different answer than one asking a long-dead healer about a wound. A Prime asking a senior mage of seven generations back about a curse receives the answer that mage would have given when they were alive, weighted by what the bones still know. Building these relationships across a lifetime is the substance of Prime mage-craft — not the casting of spells but the patient cultivation of knowing which dead to ask, and how to ask, and what they will give.

  

The magic itself is rarely showy. An ancestor-worked spell looks like a Prime sitting in stillness, sometimes speaking quietly, while something happens — a wound closes overnight, a question is answered, a missing object is found, a stranger's intentions become clear, a curse unmakes itself, a structure that was about to fall stands another century. The effects are precise, durable, and frequently inexplicable to outside observers. A wizard watching a Prime work cannot tell what was done or how, and this is a permanent source of frustration for the wizard tradition, which prizes legibility and reproducibility above almost everything else.

  

The art is suited to certain kinds of work and not to others. It does well with knowledge — any question that has been answered before by someone whose bones the mage can access. It does well with healing, particularly the long slow healing that other magics cannot manage. It does well with divination, true-naming, untangling curses, finding what is hidden, judging truth and intention, and working with time at long scales. Primes are the people one goes to when the wizards have failed, the witches have failed, and the problem is older or stranger than ordinary magic can solve.

  

What it does not do is throw fireballs, freeze armies, or level walls. A Prime in a fight cannot win the way a battle-mage of any tradition can. What a Prime can do is predict the fight's course before it begins, prepare the ground so their side has every advantage, heal their allies between blows in ways no other healer can match, and lay the kind of slow curse on an enemy that ensures even a victorious enemy loses the war that follows. Primes are not battle-mages. Primes are why one does not go to war with their monasteries.

## **The Cost of the Road**

Ancestor magic requires preparation, relationship, and place. A Prime mage in their own monastery, with full access to their bone-vaults and the proper rites prepared, is enormously powerful. A Prime mage on the road, far from their bones, is a fraction of that — competent but reduced, working from memory and the small portable relics they carry.

  

These portable relics are the ancestors a mage has the closest relationships with — a finger-bone, a tooth, a fragment of jaw, sometimes set into jewelry, sometimes carried in a pouch worn against the skin. They are named characters in their own right. A traveling Prime may carry three or four such relics: a master-teacher who taught the basics, a clan elder who answers questions of judgment, a healer of the family line whose hands the mage learned from. To lose one is the loss of a person, and the bonds are not easily replaced. A Prime who returns to their monastery without a portable relic returns having lost something they cannot recover by ordinary means.

  

This gives Primes a particular shape in the world. The greatest of them are encountered at home, where their full power is brought to bear. The Primes in the wider world — envoys, traveling scholars, healers on circuit, the rare wandering monk — are real but reduced, formidable in their own right but never operating at their full strength. Other races learn this and adjust their expectations. Approaching a Prime monastery is a different experience from meeting a Prime envoy at a foreign court.

## **Knowledge Across Millennia**

The compounding of Prime knowledge across generations is the single fact that explains most of what the rest of the continent finds intolerable about them.

  

When a Prime mage dies, they become accessible to the next generation as an ancestor. A young Prime mage can consult, in chained sequence, the master who taught them, the master who taught their master, and on backward through their lineage for as far as the bones reach. This is not legend or scripture but living testimony. A young Prime astronomer asking a question of their craft receives the considered answer of every senior astronomer in their line, weighted and refined across however many centuries the lineage has held.

  

The result is that the Primes know what no one else knows, and they can recover knowledge that everyone else has lost. Their oldest ancestor still consultable at the central shrine of the greatest monastery was alive during the Ensoulment. With proper rite, a skilled mage can ask that ancestor what was seen. The perspective is fragmentary, foreign, partial — bones do not think the way the living do — but it is real testimony from a witness no one else can call. Avenoria's working is not, to the Primes, history. It is something they can ask the dead about.

## **The Wizard Colleges**

The wizard colleges are afraid of the Primes. Quietly, and with the kind of fear that produces careful diplomacy rather than open conflict.

  

The colleges have spent centuries constructing a version of human magical history that serves their interests — a narrative in which the wizard tradition is older, more rigorous, and more consequential than the historical record actually supports. The Primes have the historical record. They have the witnesses. They do not particularly care what humans want their history to be, and they will answer questions truthfully when asked, which is the precise behavior that makes them dangerous to an institution built on a curated past.

  

The colleges manage this through a long, careful diplomatic effort: ensuring that Primes have no reason to interfere, that Prime scholars passing through human territories find their archives quietly closed to them, that the questions which would cause trouble are not asked in the rooms where the wrong people might hear the answers. The effort occasionally fails. A curious Prime envoy starts asking the wrong questions at the wrong archive, an old Prime ancestor is consulted about a matter the colleges had hoped to keep buried, and a centuries-old institutional position cracks in a way that the colleges then spend a generation repairing.

  

Beneath this, the colleges run their own counter-effort: building independent archives, conducting independent scholarship, and trying to construct a knowledge base that does not depend on Prime cooperation. They are not bad at it. Human wizards have made real discoveries. But they are always working downhill against an older, deeper tradition that they cannot access and will not acknowledge needing, and the senior scholars of the colleges know this even when they will not admit it in their own halls.

## **The Grave-Robbing**

Prime bones are valuable in ways most other races can barely imagine, and the black market in stolen relics has existed for nearly as long as the relationship with humans has.

  

The trade is run mostly by humans, supplying a small clientele of unscrupulous wizards who try to reverse-engineer ancestor magic. They cannot make it work. The magic requires the relationship, the rite, the lineage of acknowledgment that runs from the living mage back through the bones; a stolen relic is just a bone, with no one inside it willing to answer. The wizards keep trying anyway, partly out of greed and partly out of the institutional refusal to accept that there is a magical tradition older than theirs which they will never access.

  

The Primes do not forgive this. Several humans across the centuries have died unpleasantly at significant distance from any Prime, of causes that no investigator could ever trace. Children of grave-robbing families have died in their cradles. Wizards working with stolen relics have lost the use of their hands. A merchant house implicated in the trade in the previous century lost three generations of male heirs in succession, none of them to anything an investigator would call magical. The Primes do not comment on these matters, do not claim them, and would not respond to the question if asked. The pattern is consistent enough that the trade has survived only because the prices are extraordinary and the buyers are wealthy enough to absorb the losses.

## **The Wartide and the Withdrawal**

The Primes did not fight the Wartide alongside Avenoria. This is the political fact that has shaped their position in human Hedoria for centuries, and the question of why is the question every Prime mage of conscience eventually has to answer.

  

There are two accounts. They do not entirely contradict each other.

  

The first is the account the Primes give of themselves, when pressed. Ancestor magic is bound to place. The bone-vaults are anchored. The full power of a Prime mage is not portable, and the war Avenoria fought was a war of mass force-creation across a continent — not a war the Primes' tradition was structurally capable of joining at the scale she needed. Their healing was offered. Their counsel was offered. Their archives were opened to her envoys. What was not offered was an army of monastery-mages walking down from the mountains to throw themselves into a battle their tradition had no method for. The Primes did what they could within their nature. They did not become something they were not. The criticism that they should have, in this account, is a criticism that does not understand what was being asked.

  

The second account is the one carried in Avenor's deepest archives, and increasingly the one the young, ardent Primes themselves have begun to make. Avenoria asked. The exact words of the refusal are preserved. The Primes had ancestors who could have gone with field-mages in portable form. They had healing the war desperately needed and gave it sparingly. They had monasteries' worth of senior mages who could have committed to the cause and chose preservation instead. The "could not" is the comforting story. The truth is "would not." Humans died in numbers because the most powerful magical tradition on the continent chose its archives over the war it was being asked to help fight, and the Primes have lived with the consequences of that choice ever since — including the centuries of human resentment that they cannot fully answer.

  

Both accounts are partly true. The Prime tradition genuinely is anchored, conservative, and oriented toward preservation. It also genuinely could have done more than it did. Whether that constitutes betrayal depends on whether one believes a people can make themselves into something other than what they are, and the Primes have been arguing about that question internally for as long as the question has existed.

## **The Bone-Vaults Are Conservative**

What makes the question structurally difficult, and not merely a historical grievance, is that the ancestors who made the withdrawal decision are still in the bone-vaults. They are still consulted. They are still part of the magical chain that every working Prime mage draws on.

  

The high monastery councils of the Wartide era — the senior figures who voted to refuse Avenoria's envoys — are interred in the great cliff-vaults of the central monasteries. Their judgment is in the magic itself. A Prime ancestor-mage invoking a senior of that generation is invoking someone who chose the archives over Avenoria. The decision is not abstract history, and it is not safely sealed away in the past. It is present in every working that draws on those ancestors' will and knowledge.

  

Younger Prime scholars who want to commit to the second Wartide find that the ancestral counsel they receive — when they invoke the elders who shaped the withdrawal — is consistent. The old ancestors still believe they were right. Their reasoning has not changed. They counsel preservation, patience, withholding. To work against their counsel is to work against the deep grain of one's own magical tradition. Some young Primes do it anyway. Some find that the magic resists — that workings aimed at active engagement with worldly affairs come more slowly, more reluctantly, than workings aimed at preservation. The bone-vaults themselves are conservative because the bones are conservative, and there is no overruling them by majority vote of the living.

  

This means the Primes' commitment to the second Wartide cannot be a simple institutional decision. It is a multi-generational struggle within their own ancestral magic. The young, ardent Primes are arguing not just with their living elders but with their dead ones, and the dead ones are the ones who hold the magic. Even the most committed Prime warrior-monk in the field carries, in the relics drawn from his monastery, the will of ancestors who would have him retreat. He works against his own bones.

## **Avenor, and the Silence**

The historical relationship with Avenor was the closest thing to peerage the Primes had with any other power. The Avenorian crown maintained permanent embassies at the three greatest monasteries. Avenorian witches were occasionally permitted to apprentice with Prime ancestor-mages, learning what they could of the older art. The relationship was epistemic as much as diplomatic — Avenor's foreign policy was sharper than the other human kingdoms' partly because the witch queens consulted the people who actually remembered, and the Primes valued Avenor as the only human power that had not built its self-image on a lie about its own magical history.

  

Both sides understood the limits of the alliance. The Primes warned, but rarely committed. Avenor received the warnings, but did not assume warning would translate into help. They cooperated where they could and did not pretend to a warmth that the underlying facts could not support.

  

That arrangement, such as it was, has gone quiet in the present generation.

  

The witch queen is no longer the queen the Primes knew. Whatever has hold of Sephilia has reordered her priorities, and the priority that now overrides all the others is the prevention of a third soul-war between the races. The Prime warnings about the second Wartide cut directly across that priority — a queen focused on holding the racial peace does not want to hear that the real threat is older and that the races must commit together to fighting it. Prime envoys arriving in Highcoven are still received with the full apparatus of the historical alliance. Their warnings are heard. They are politely set aside. The senior witches who would once have pressed the queen to act on them are exiled, retired, or dead, and the heir who saw clearly is somewhere in the world without standing.

  

The Primes are alone in a way they have not been in centuries.

  

This has consequences inside the monasteries that the human kingdoms do not see. The young, ardent Primes who want their tradition to commit have been losing the internal argument for years already; the silence from Avenor is now being cited by the conservatives as confirmation. *Even the witch queen does not act. The world is not ready. The archives are what we have to give. Preserve them.* The bone-vaults agree. The young scholars who pushed for engagement find their workings slower, their ancestral counsel more uniformly resistant, the institutional weight of their own tradition pressing them back toward the long view. Some continue to push anyway. Most quietly accept that they have lost this generation's argument and turn their attention to preparing for the next.

  

What this means in practice is that the Primes will document the second Wartide. They will warn whoever will listen. They will keep their archives, refine their workings, deepen their bone-vaults, and watch. If the world burns, their monasteries will likely survive — they survived the first Wartide and the Unmade era and they are designed to survive — and the Primes who emerge on the far side will know, in the ancestral record, exactly what happened and why. This is what their tradition is for. It is not what the world needs from them. It is what they will give.

## **The Lost Vaults**

Some of the great pre-ensoulment vaults were destroyed or lost during the Unmade era. The bones of the oldest Primes — the ones who would remember most, the ones who were ancient when Avenoria was young — are gone, scattered, or hidden in places no living Prime knows how to find.

  

Recovering a lost vault is the kind of multi-generational project the Primes pursue patiently across centuries. Expeditions are mounted, fail, and are mounted again a generation later with what has been learned. A vault successfully recovered is a major event in Prime institutional life, restoring access to ancestors who have been silent for an era. There are persistent rumors that a pre-ensoulment vault has been discovered intact in the deep north. If the rumor is true, and if the vault can be reached and opened safely, what comes out of it would change what the world remembers.

  

The Primes are aware of the rumor. They are taking it seriously. They are not commenting publicly on what they are doing about it.
